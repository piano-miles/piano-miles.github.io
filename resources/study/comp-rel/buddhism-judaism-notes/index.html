Buddhism and Judaism

Buddhism

Q: What was Siddhartha Gautama born as?

A: Siddhartha Gautama was born as a prince.

Q: How old was he until he went outside and saw the “four sights”?

A: Siddhartha Gautama was 29 years old when he saw the "four sights" for the first time.

Q: What are the “four sights”?

A: The "four sights" are four encounters that Siddhartha had with an old man, a sick man, a dead man, and a holy man, which led him torenounce his luxurious life and seek enlightenment.

  • “Old age, sickness, death, and a wanderingascetic”
  • Ascetic: a person whopracticessevere self-discipline and abstention.

Q: What was Siddhartha's interest in solving?

A: Siddhartha was interested in solving the problem of suffering and finding a way to overcome it.

  • Believed that individual humans were responsible forsuffering as well as finding a solution to suffering

Q: What path did Siddhartha choose during his years of wandering?

A: During his years of wandering, Siddhartha Gautama followed the path ofasceticism and extreme self-denial.

Q: At what age did Siddhartha reach enlightenment?

A: Siddhartha Gautama reached enlightenment at the age of 35.

Q: What does “The Buddha” mean?

A: "The Buddha" means "the enlightened one" or "the awakened one."

Q: What is the “Triple Refuge”?

A: "The Triple Refuge" is a fundamental concept in Buddhism that refers totakingrefuge in the Buddha, the Dharma (the teachings), and the Sangha (the community of followers).

  • “Is a place one goes to in order to find peace, security,andsafety.”
  • Three vows
  • I take refuge in the Buddha.
  • I take refuge in the dharma.
  • I take refuge in the sangha.

Q: What was Siddhartha’s first sermon titled?

A: Siddhartha's first sermon was titled "Setting in Motion the Wheel of theDharma"or "Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta."

Q: What are “stupas”?

A: "Stupas" are dome-shaped structures that contain Buddhist relics orrepresent important events or teachings in Buddhism.

  • Buddhist stupas were originally built to house theearthly remains of the historical Buddha and hisassociates.

Q: How can one “take refuge in the Buddha’s experience”?

A: One can take refuge in the Buddha's experience by following his teachings andapplying them to their own life, seeking to attain the same level ofenlightenment and wisdom that the Buddha achieved.

  • Taking refuge is done by a short formula in which onenames the Buddha, the dharma, and thesaṅghaas refuges.

Q: What are the “4 Noble Truths”?

A: The "4 Noble Truths" are the foundation of Buddhistteachings and state that:

1) suffering exists,

2) suffering arises from craving andattachment,

3) suffering can be overcome andended, and

4) the path totheend of suffering is the Eightfold Path.

Q: What is the Sangha?

A: The Sangha is the community of Buddhistpractitioners who follow the Buddha's teachings and support each otherintheir spiritual practice.

Q: What are the “3 poisons”?

A: The "3 poisons" in Buddhism refer to the negative mental states ofignorance, attachment, and aversion, which are seen asthe root causesof suffering.

  1. Ignorance
  2. Strong attachment
  3. Strong dislike or disinclination (aversion)

Q: How many years after the Buddha died did a dispute take place to splitBuddhism?

A: A dispute took place to split Buddhism about 100years after the Buddha's death.

Q: What are the two splits in Buddhism?

A: The two main splits in Buddhism are Theravada (the Way of the Elders) and Mahayana (the Great Vehicle).

Q: In the 8th century, two schools of Buddhism held a debate—the Chinese andtheIndian school. Which one was chosen by The King of Tibet?

A: The King of Tibet chose the Indian school of Buddhism over the Chinese school after a debate held in the 8th century.

Judaism

Q: How many Jews are there globally today?

A: According to recent estimates, there are approximately 14.7 (2021) ~ 15.3 (2023) million Jewsworldwide.

Q: How many gods do Jews worship? …lol

A: Judaism is a monotheistic religion, which means that Jews believe inone God.

Q: What is the “Shema” a reference to?

A: The "Shema" is a prayer thatis a central affirmation of the Jewish faith,declaring the oneness of God and the duty to love and serve Him with all one'sheart, soul,and strength.

  • It was a daily prayer for ancient Israelites and is stillrecited by Jewish people today.

Q: What is the formal name for God in Judaism?

A: The formal name for God in Judaism is "Yahweh," which is often rendered as "Adonai" in Jewish liturgy andscripture.

Q: What is a general name for God in Genesis?

A: In Genesis, God is often referred to as "Elohim," which is a Hebrew wordthatmeans "God" or "mighty one."

Q: What does the word “Adonai” translate to?

A: The word "Adonai" is a Hebrew word that means "Lord"or"Master," and is used as a title for God in Jewish liturgy andscripture.

Q: What did “Jacob’s” name change to?

A: Jacob's name was changed to "Israel" after he wrestled with an angel in Genesis.

  • He demonstrated that he was willing to let God prevail inhislife. In response, God changed Jacob's name to Israel, meaning ‘let God prevail’.

Q: What is the Tanakh?

A: The Tanakh is the Hebrew Bible,which consists of three main sections: the Torah, the Prophets (Nevi'im), and the Writings(Ketuvim).

  • Tanakh is an acronym derived from the names of the three divisions of the Hebrew Bible:
  • Torah (Instruction, or Law, also called thePentateuch),
  •  * The Torah contains fivebooks: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy.
  • Neviʾim (Prophets), and
  •  * The Neviʾim comprises eightbooks subdivided into:
  • the Former Prophets, containing the four historical works Joshua, Judges, Samuel, and Kings; and
  • the Latter Prophets, the oracular discourses of Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the Twelve (Minor)Prophets—Hosea, Joel, Amos, Obadiah, Jonah, Micah, Nahum, Habakkuk, Zephaniah, Haggai, Zechariah,andMalachi.
  • Ketuvim (Writings).
  • The Ketuvim consists of religiouspoetry and wisdom literature
  • Psalms, Proverbs, and Job, a collection known as the“FiveMegillot”
  • (“scrolls”; i.e., Song of Songs, Ruth, Lamentations,Ecclesiastes, and Esther, which have been grouped together according to the annual cycle of theirpublicreading in the synagogue)
  • and the books of Daniel, Ezra, Nehemiah, andChronicles.

Q: What does “Torah” mean?

A: The word "Torah" literally means "instruction" or"teaching," and refers to the first fivebooks of the Hebrew Bible, which are considered the foundational text ofJudaism.

Q: What does “Nevi’im” mean?

A: "Nevi'im" is a Hebrew word that means "prophets," and refers to the secondsection of the Hebrew Bible.

Q: What does “Kethuvim” mean?

A: "Kethuvim" is a Hebrew wordthatmeans "writings," and refers to the third section oftheHebrew Bible.

Q: How did Yahweh confirm his relationship with his people?

A: According to Jewish tradition, Yahweh confirmed his relationship with hispeoplethrough a covenant at Mount Sinai, which was a mutual agreement between God and the Israelites to follow hiscommandments.

Q: What two things did God promise Abraham?

A: God promised Abraham two things: that he would have manydescendants, and that his descendants would inherit thelandof Canaan.

Q: Which character is the focus of the book of Exodus?

A: The book of Exodus primarily focuses on the character of Moses, who led the Israelites out of slavery in Egypt and receivedtheTen Commandments from God on Mount Sinai.

Q: What was the name of the mountain that the Torah was given?

A: The mountain on which the Torah was given is called MountSinai, also known as Mount Horeb.

Q: Which book describes the establishment of the Kingdom of Israel?

A: The book of Samuel describes the establishment of the Kingdom of Israel, including the anointing of thefirst king, Saul, and the reign of King David.

Q: Where did David make his Capital city?

A: David made his capital city in Jerusalem.

Q: Who built the temple of God?

A: The first temple of God in Jerusalem was built by KingSolomon (990–931 BC), the son of David.

Q: Who destroyed the Northern Kingdom of Israel in 721 BC?

A: The Northern Kingdom of Israel was destroyed by the Assyrian Empire in 721 BC.

Q: Who destroyed the Southern Kingdom of Judah in 586 BC?

A: The Southern Kingdom of Judah was destroyed by the Babylonian Empire in 586 BC, and the Jewish people wereexiled toBabylon for several decades.
