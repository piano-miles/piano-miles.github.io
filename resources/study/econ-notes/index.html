Factors of Production

Land is any natural resource used to produce goods and services.

Labor is the human effort that can be applied to the production of goods and services. The effort expended by an individual to bring a product or service to the market.

  • Skilled and trained workers are called human capital.

Capital (investment) is the machinery, tools and buildings humans use to produce goods and services (e.g. hammers, forklifts, conveyer belts, computers, delivery vans).

Entrepreneurship combines all the other factors of production into a product or service for the consumer market. It is the activity of setting up a business or businesses, taking on financial risks in the hope of profit.

Money is not a factor of production as it is not directly involved in producing a good or service.

Demand schedule

  • Table or Chart of all quantities demanded at each price.

Demand=Marginal Benefit

Demand Curve

  • Shows price willing and able to pay at each quantity.

Demand slopes downward

  • Reflects diminishing marginal utility

  • Only be willing to buy more shirts if the price goes down.

  • Income effect (Purchasing power)

  • If you do not produce sufficient income, you will not be inclined to buy as much.
  • When the price of a good falls, consumers experience an increase in purchasing power. When the price of a good increases, consumers experience a decrease in purchasing power.

  • Substitution effect

  • When an item is priced high, people will turn to find substitutes.
  • When the price of a good changes, consumers will substitute toward the now relatively less-expensive good.

RIPEN: Shifting Demand

  • Related Goods: price of substitutes and compliments (see “Related Goods” section below)
  • Income (disposable income)
  • Income increases=more Demand because people have more money and are therefore willing to buy more
  • Preferences: ads, seasons…
  • Beachwear Demand is high in the summer, low in the winter
  • Expectations of Future Price
  • You want to buy it when it’s low! If people expect the price to go up, they’ll buy more now. If they expect the price to drop, they’ll buy less now and wait for the price to drop.
  • Number of Buyers
  • More buyers in the market=more Demand for the products

Shifting Right (increasing)

Related Goods

  • Substitute
  • A similar or competing good
  • Example: Coffee and Tea
  • Price up → Demand up!
  • Compliment
  • A good that goes with another
  • Example: iTunes and iPods
  • Price up → Demand down, inverse effect.

Demand vs. QD

  • Changing the price of the good DOES NOT SHIFT the Demand curve for that good.
  • Changing the price of the good will only shift where you are on the curve.
  • Shifters will shift the Demand curve, but the curve relates the Demand to the price.

Price Elasticity

  • Price elasticity measures the responsiveness of the quantity demanded or supplied of a good to a change in its price.
  • A measurement of the change in the consumption of a product in relation to a change in its price

 

(proportional over the course of the price change)

  • If price elasticity is greater than 1, the good is elastic; if less than 1, it is inelastic; if it is equal to 1, it is unit elastic / unitary. If it is infinity, it is perfectly elastic, if it is 0, it is perfectly inelastic.
  • Used to understand how supply and demand for a product change when its price changes

Determinants of Price Elasticity

  • Luxury vs. necessity
  • Proportion of income
  • Number of available substitutes
  • Time period (how much time you have to consider alternatives)

Total Revenue Test

  • Total Revenue =Price  Quantity
  • P up, TR down=elastic
  • P up, TR up=inelastic
  • P up, TR same=unit elastic

Price Floors / Ceilings

Ceiling

  • Legal maximum price
  • Occurs below the equilibrium point
  • If it is placed above the equilibrium point, it will have no effect
  • Represented with a solid line
  • E.g., rent control, electricity
  • Creates an artificial shortage

Floor

  • Legal minimum price
  • E.g., minimum wage (labor price)
  • Creates an artificial surplus
  • Occurs above the equilibrium point
  • If it is placed below the equilibrium point, it will have no effect

Subsidies

  • A sum of money granted by the government or a public body to assist an industry or business so that the price of a commodity or service may remain low or competitive.
  • Increases supply, resulting in a decrease in price and an increase in equilibrium quantity.

Gross Domestic Product

  • The dollar amount of all final goods and services produced in a country.
  • GDP =total produced market value.

 What is not included in GDP

  • Black market (not legal)
  • Abroad (Not “Made in America”)
  • Nonproduction transactions (things that you pay for that are not goods or services)
  • Buying stocks, exchanging money
  • Double counting
  • Intermediate goods (goods that go into making a product)
  • E.g., We wouldn’t include every single component making up the laptop, we just count the entire laptop.
  • Secondhand sales (used)

How GDP is Measured

  • Measured through spending
  • Consumer: you and me
  • Investment: businesses
  • Government (): school
  • Net Exports (X-M): exported – imported

Real vs. Nominal GDP

  • Nominal GDP: Current dollar GDP (Today’s P Today’s Q)
  • Real GDP:GDP adjusted for inflation (Original P Today’s Q)

GDP Deflator

  • Measure of the change in price level (inflation) calculated as the ratio of Nominal GDP  to Real GDP  (times 100).
  • Measure of the level of prices of all new, domestically produced, final goods and services in an economy in one year.

Inflation

  • A rise in the general level of prices.
  • Reported via the CPI or implicit GDP price deflator
  • Inflation increases at the end of expansions, and decreases in periods of recession
  • Deflation is a decrease in the general price level

Inflation categories

  • Creeping inflation
  • Gradual inflation up to 3% per year
  • Galloping inflation
  • Intense inflation, 100%-300% per year
  • Hyperinflation
  • 500%+ inflation per year, the stage before total monetary collapse of an economy.

Causes of Inflation

Demand-pull Inflation

Aggregate demand increases for pro- ducts because people have higher wages, and they can afford to pay higher prices for more products.

Cost-push Inflation

Increased aggregate demand causes the cost of important goods or services to increase where no suitable alternative is available, people want higher wages to compensate for the higher prices, and aggregate supply curve decreases as a result.

  • Increases in the cost of inputs, especially labor, drive up the cost of products for manufacturers.
  • A self-perpetuating spiral of wages and prices can increase inflation. Workers demand more wages to compensate for higher prices, and employers need to raise wages to cover increased costs of labor.
  • Excessive monetary growth is the final reason for inflation. This happens when the money supply grows faster than real GDP. When the Federal Reserve increases money supply, some people will have more money to spend and this will lead to increased prices.

* Inflation is normal and healthy for the economy.

As long as wages can keep up, it is not bad.

  • The literal amount of money you get paid per hour or by salary.
  • Real wage is the real amount of money.

* When inflation occurs, purchasing power decreases.

Consequences of Inflation

  • Fixed Income Receivers

  • Inflation can have a negative effect on fixed-income assets when it leads to higher interest rates.
  • Party receiving a fixed-rate payment stream profits if interest rates fall, and loses if interest rates rise.

  • Public Sector Workers

  • Public sector workers have fixed income, so they are negatively impacted by inflation. The ‘fixed’ steps may not keep up with inflation.

  • Savers

  • As prices rise, the real value / purchasing power of an accumulated savings deteriorates.
  • Money you save is worth less when prices inflate.

  • Creditors

  • The bank loans Mr. Waugh $1000 to be repaid in 2 years. Price levels double. The $1000 repayment has only ½ the purchasing power it did before. The bank is negatively affected.
  • Interest
  • Inflation causes interest rates to go up, which causes people to buy less durable goods.

1)  Frictional Unemployment

  • “A kind of unemployment caused by friction in the economy”
  • Transitional, “in between jobs”
  • E.g., if you are in college looking for a job, it is transitional, you are going to become a productive member of society. It’s natural
  • E.g., quitting to find a better job: the economy is doing well enough that people can quit willingly and feel confident that they can find a better job.

2)  Structural Unemployment

  • Changes in demand or technology
  • Skills no longer matches demands of the industry
  • E.g., car assemblyman is replaced by a machine.
  • E.g., artists are replaced by Dall•E 2.
  • Not considered bad necessarily, but it can be.

3)  Cyclical Unemployment

  • Due tophases in the business cycle
  • E.g., Netflix lost 50 million subscribers: we need to cut down workforce
  • This is the “bad” kind of unemployment
  • E.g.: Current tech
  • E.g.: Company merges with another or gets bought out. This tends to fit more in structural unemployment, but there could be an overlap.

4)  Seasonal Unemployment

  • Changing needs in the industry
  • E.g., The Christmas tree market is busier during Christmas shopping time, not as much in the summer.
  • E.g., Farming: if you are in a harvest time, you hire more workers during that time.
  • Seasonal demands

Full Employment

  • We will NEVER reach a 0% unemployment rate.
  • You will always have the result of frictional and structural unemployment.
  • The best unemployment rate is not 0%—we estimate it to be around 3%. 3% unemployment is the objective for the economy, called the natural rate of unemployment (ERU).
  • The natural rate of unemployment does not include cyclical unemployment, because cyclical unemployment is bad.

3 Tools of the Fed

Fed controls the money supply

  • More Money Supply=Expansionary
  • Lower Money Supply=Contractionary
  • Too much money → debasement
  • Balance between maintaining growth and avoiding inflation

3 tools:

  1. Open Market Operations
  2. Reserve Requirement
  3. The Discount Rate

Open Market Operations

  • Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) buys and sells bonds. (Treasuries / T-Bills / Securities)
  1. Buy Bonds: Fed pays money to people / banks=higher Excess Reserves=more loans (Expansionary)
  2. Sell Bonds: People/banks pay money to Fed (Contractionary)
  • Excess Reserve=Total Reserve – Required Reserve

The Discount Rate

  • The interest rate the Fed charges on loans to banks.
  • Lower rate: Banks can borrow easily=ER up=more loans=MS up (Expansionary)
  • Higher rate: Banks can’t borrow or lend as easily=MS Down (Contractionary)

Reserve Requirement

  • RR: The percent of deposits that a bank must keep on reserve.
  • High RR=low excess reserves=few loans=MS down.
  • Higher %=Contract MS
  • Lower %=Expand MS
